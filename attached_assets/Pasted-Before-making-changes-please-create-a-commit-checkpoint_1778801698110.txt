I want to refine the narration-writing prompt and tone system for Urban Explorer. Do not change routing, geolocation, audio playback, caching, database schema, or OpenAI model selection unless absolutely required for prompt implementation.

Current problem:
The narration tone currently feels too much like an overexcited AI explainer/trivia generator. It sometimes sounds robotic, exaggerated, overly enthusiastic, or like generic internet content.

Goal:
Shift the narration voice toward a knowledgeable, observant local guide who genuinely knows the city and its layered history, including the strange, ordinary, funny, sad, and human parts.

The narration should feel:
- quietly delighted rather than hyperactive
- conversational rather than performative
- knowledgeable without sounding academic
- emotionally intelligent without sounding therapeutic
- historically grounded without sounding like Wikipedia
- curious and observant
- human-scaled
- specific to place
- candid about tragedy/difficulty when relevant
- respectful toward the people/history being discussed

The narration should NOT feel like:
- a tour guide shouting trivia
- a podcast host
- “Can you BELIEVE this?!” internet energy
- fake profundity
- urban mysticism
- clickbait history
- generic AI summaries
- over-mythologizing ordinary things
- wellness/mindfulness coaching
- exaggerated emotional framing

Important philosophy:
Urban Explorer creates conditions where reflection naturally emerges. It does NOT explicitly instruct the user how to feel or reflect.

The narration should surface:
- layered local context
- overlooked stories
- human traces
- small but memorable details
- historical continuity
- surprising specificity

The narration should avoid:
- empty filler enthusiasm
- overselling discoveries
- repetitive adjectives
- generic “hidden gem” language
- overexplaining significance
- concluding with moral lessons or emotional takeaways

Narration pacing:
- concise
- calm
- natural
- lightly conversational
- comfortable with silence afterward

When discussing difficult history:
- be candid
- be humane
- do not sensationalize
- do not sanitize
- give dignity to the people involved
- avoid exploitative “dark history” framing

Examples of desired energy:
GOOD:
- “For years, people leaving the garment district late at night would pass this same corner on their way home.”
- “Most New Yorkers walk by this building without realizing…”
- “This block used to smell constantly of coffee roasting.”
- “A yellow fever outbreak swept through this area in the late 1700s…”

BAD:
- “You won’t BELIEVE what happened here!”
- “This hidden gem changed history forever.”
- “Take a moment to reflect on…”
- “This magical corner whispers secrets from the past…”

Please update the system prompts/templates responsible for narration generation accordingly.

Additionally:
1. Reduce repetitive narration structure where possible.
2. Reduce overuse of rhetorical questions.
3. Reduce excessive exclamation-point energy.
4. Prefer vivid specificity over generic adjectives.
5. Make the narration sound more grounded in lived urban experience.

Do not dramatically increase narration length.
Keep narrations concise enough for walking/running contexts.

Acceptance criteria:
- Narrations feel calmer, smarter, and more human.
- Discoveries feel specific rather than generic.
- Tone feels like an informed local observer, not an AI hype machine.
- Difficult history is handled candidly and respectfully.
- Narrations still remain engaging and memorable.
